## title: "Alpha Reading Fluency"

Alpha Reading Fluency is a **reading app for young students** focused on the foundational skill of reading **accurately and smoothly out loud**. The app listens as your child reads aloud and provides real-time support, building the automatic decoding that makes later reading comprehension possible. It is designed with early and developing readers in mind, including built-in accessibility for students with dyslexia.

In simple terms: this is the **"learn to read"** stage — decoding and oral fluency — that comes _before_ a student moves on to comprehension-focused work.

## What It Is

- **Oral reading fluency.** Students read words and passages aloud; the app uses speech technology to listen, give feedback, and help correct mistakes in the moment.
- **Built for early and struggling readers.** A friendly, kid-focused experience that meets each child at their level.
- **Dyslexia-friendly by design.** The app uses the **OpenDyslexic** typeface to make text easier to read for students with dyslexia.

## How We Can Improve the Learning Experience

<Frame caption="Alpha Language K–2 — build-the-word activity. The child spells the word into the empty boxes using the letter tiles below.">
  ![Alpha Reading Fluency Alpha Language K2](/images/alpha_reading_fluency_alpha_language_k2.jpg)
</Frame>

This activity asks the child to **build a word** from letter tiles. It already includes **audio instructions**, and the interaction itself is intuitive. The opportunity is to **pair that audio with visual cues** — especially for K–2 students, who are still building listening stamina and can easily miss or forget a spoken prompt:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="A replayable sound cue" icon="volume-high">
    Keep the audio, but add a clear **🔊 "hear the word" button** above the boxes so the child can replay the prompt any time — not just once at the start.
  </Card>

  <Card title="A visual cue" icon="image">
    Add a **picture of the target word** (for example, a map or a cat) alongside the audio. A visual prompt lets the child see the task as well as hear it, and supports pre-readers who can't yet rely on spoken instructions alone.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Note>
  The audio already gives children one way in — a matching **visual cue gives them a second**. For K–2 learners, seeing _and_ hearing the prompt makes the activity far easier to follow independently.
</Note>
